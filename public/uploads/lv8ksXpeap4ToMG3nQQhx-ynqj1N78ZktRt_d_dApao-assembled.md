# Bearing Stress Calculation and Stress-Strain Curve

## Bearing Stress Calculation

The bearing stress is defined as:

$$ \sigma_{\text{Bearing}} = \frac{\text{load}}{\text{Projected Area}} $$

Given: Load = $35 \times 10^3$ N

$$ 7 \times \frac{N}{mm^2} = \frac{35 \times 10^3 N}{L \times D} $$

$$ \frac{7}{mm^2} = \frac{35 \times 10^3}{1.2 \times D} $$

$$ \frac{7}{mm^2} = \frac{35 \times 10^3}{1.2 \times D^2} $$

$$ D^2 = \frac{35 \times 10^3}{1.2 \times 7} \text{ mm}^2 $$

$$ D^2 = \frac{5 \times 10^4}{12} \text{ mm}^2 $$

$$ D = \sqrt{\frac{5}{12} \times 10^4 \text{ mm}^2} = 64.549 \text{ mm} $$

Therefore,
$$ D = 64.55 \text{ mm} $$

And $L = 1.2 D = 1.2 \times 64.55 \text{ mm} = 77.46 \text{ mm}$

## Bidirectional Stresses

[DIAGRAM: A 3D cube with normal stresses $\sigma_x$ and $\sigma_y$ acting on opposite faces, and shear stresses $\tau_{xy}$ acting on other faces. Arrows indicate the directions of the stresses.]

[DIAGRAM: A 2D square element with normal stresses $\sigma_x$ and $\sigma_y$ acting on opposite faces, and shear stresses $\tau_{xy}$ acting on adjacent faces. Arrows indicate the directions of the stresses.]

The general equations for principal stresses $\sigma_{1,2}$ in a 2D stress state are:

$$ \sigma_{1,2} = \frac{1}{2}(\sigma_x + \sigma_y) \pm \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{(\sigma_x - \sigma_y)^2 + 4\tau_{xy}^2} $$

The radius of the Mohr's circle, $\tau$, is given by:

$$ \tau = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{(\sigma_x - \sigma_y)^2 + 4\tau_{xy}^2} $$

### For Unidirectional Stresses

In the case of unidirectional stresses, the equation simplifies. If we assume $\tau_{xy} = 0$ and $\sigma_y = 0$, the formula becomes:

$$ \sigma = \frac{\sigma_x}{2} \pm \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\sigma_x^2} = \frac{\sigma_x}{2} \pm \frac{\sigma_x}{2} $$

This gives $\sigma_1 = \sigma_x$ and $\sigma_2 = 0$.

The general form of the equation for unidirectional stresses (where $\tau_{xy} = 0$) is:

$$ \sigma = \frac{\sigma}{2} \pm \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\sigma^2 + 4\tau^2} $$
[UNCLEAR: The symbol $\sigma$ on the right side of the equation appears to be intended to represent $\sigma_x$.]

### Maximum and Minimum Principal Stresses

The maximum principal stress ($\sigma_{\text{max}}$) and minimum principal stress ($\sigma_{\text{min}}$) can be calculated as:

$$ \sigma_{\text{max}} = \frac{1}{2}(\sigma_x + \sigma_y) + \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{(\sigma_x - \sigma_y)^2 + 4\tau_{xy}^2} $$

$$ \sigma_{\text{min}} = \frac{1}{2}(\sigma_x + \sigma_y) - \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{(\sigma_x - \sigma_y)^2 + 4\tau_{xy}^2} $$

These can also be expressed in terms of the average normal stress ($\sigma_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\sigma_x+\sigma_y}{2}$) and the radius of the Mohr's circle ($\tau$):

$$ \sigma_{\text{max}} = \sigma_{\text{avg}} + \tau $$

$$ \sigma_{\text{min}} = \sigma_{\text{avg}} - \tau $$

### Maximum Shear Stress

The maximum shear stress ($\tau_{\text{max}}$) is related to the principal stresses by:

$$ \tau_{\text{max}} = \sqrt{\sigma_{\text{avg}}^2 + \tau^2} $$

## Theories of Failure

1) **Rankine Theory** (Maximum Stress Theory/Maximum normal Stress)

Max$^m$ stress ($\sigma$ or $\sigma_1$ or $\sigma_3$ whenever is maximum) = $\sigma_y$ (yield)

Avoid failure

$\sigma_1 = \frac{\sigma_y}{FOS}$ (maximum)

Failure in cylinders occurs at a point when maximum principle stress/normal stress in a biaxial stress system reaches the maximum limiting value of material strength in a simple test.

(2) **Guest's Theory** or **Coulomb's Theory** or **Maximum Shear theory**

$$ \sigma_1 - \sigma_2 \text{ or } \sigma_2 - \sigma_3 \text{ or } \sigma_3 - \sigma_1 \left( \frac{\text{whichever is maximum}}{} \right) = \sigma_y $$

Failure or yielding occurs at a point in members when max shear in a biaxial stress system reaches the limiting value of shear in a simple tension test.

* Above theorem is applicable to ductile material.

## Theories of Failure

### St. Venant's Theory or Maximum Strain Theory

The equations for the stresses are given by:
$$
\sigma_1 - \nu(\sigma_2 + \sigma_3)
$$
$$
\sigma_2 - \nu(\sigma_1 + \sigma_3)
$$
$$
\sigma_3 - \nu(\sigma_1 + \sigma_2)
$$

These are equated to:
$$
\frac{\sigma_y}{FOS}
$$

Where:
* $\sigma_y =$ yield strength
* $\nu =$ Poisson's ratio
* FOS = factor of safety

### Maximum Strain Energy Theorems

The condition for failure is given by:
$$
\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 + \sigma_3^2 = 2\nu(\sigma_1\sigma_2 + \sigma_2\sigma_3 + \sigma_3\sigma_1) = \sigma_y^2
$$

To avoid failure:
$$
\frac{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 + \sigma_3^2 - 2\nu(\sigma_1\sigma_2 + \sigma_2\sigma_3 + \sigma_3\sigma_1)}{2} = \left(\frac{\sigma_y}{FOS}\right)^2
$$

### Octahedral or Distortion Energy Theory (Von-Mises Theory)

The equation for failure is:
$$
\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 + \sigma_3^2 - \sigma_1\sigma_2 - \sigma_2\sigma_3 - \sigma_3\sigma_1 = \sigma_y^2
$$

For permanent failure:
$$
\frac{1}{2}[(\sigma_1 - \sigma_2)^2 + (\sigma_2 - \sigma_3)^2 + (\sigma_3 - \sigma_1)^2] = \left(\frac{\sigma_y}{FOS}\right)^2
$$
